Antarctica

By Vince Harvey E. Bobiles | 1st of February, Year 2026

Why I Want to Visit Antarctica

Antarctica is the coldest and windiest continent on Earth, and it is one of the few places that still feels truly untouched by modern life. I want to experience its silence, endless ice fields, and dramatic skies that shift from soft blue to bright white in moments. The place is also home to penguins, seals, and whales, which makes it feel like a living documentary in real time. More than tourism, visiting Antarctica would be a chance to understand climate change in a place where its effects are already visible.

Quick Facts

Antarctica has no permanent cities and no native human population. Most people there are scientists and support teams working at research stations. During summer, daylight can last almost the entire day, while in winter, darkness dominates for long periods. The continent stores most of the planet's freshwater in its ice sheets. That is one reason why Antarctica is one of the most important places for global scientific research.

It is also the driest continent, which surprises many people because it is covered in ice. Winds can be extremely strong, and temperatures can drop lower than almost anywhere else on Earth. These conditions make it both beautiful and harsh at the same time. In fact, despite the ice, it is the largest desert on Earth, receiving very little precipitation annually. It is so cold that it is the only place on Earth where you can find snow all year round. No native animals live there, but it is home to a variety of birds and marine life especially penguins.

There is also no time zone in Antarctica, making it the only place on Earth where you can experience the midnight sun. And the midnight sun is not the only unique thing about Antarctica. It is also the only place on Earth where you can see the aurora australis, the southern lights.

What I Want to Experience

I want to experience seeing giant glaciers up close and hearing the sound of ice cracking in the distance. Watching penguins in their natural habitat is high on my list, especially seeing how they move and interact as a group. I also want to witness the midnight sun and the unique lighting that makes the ice look silver and blue.

I always wondered what it feels like to be so cold that you can see your breath in the air. I always wondered what it feels like to be so alone that you can hear your own heartbeat. I always wondered what it feels like to be so small that you can see the stars in the sky. I always wondered what it feels like to be so alive that you can feel the pain of the cold.

I want to feel the intense and sharp cold that penetrates to my bones and the feeling of being so small in the face of nature. Before I can say I have seen the world, that I was alive, that I have experienced the cold, the silence, the pain on this Earth, I will visit Antarctica as my last destination.
